# ISSUE 1977 — PM Term Limit Bill Fails by Two Votes: Anwar's Signature Reform Stalls in Dewan Rakyat

## PERIOD
December 2019 – April 2026

## CONTEXT (Timeline)

- **2018 Mar**: Pakatan Harapan's GE14 manifesto includes a pledge to introduce a two-term limit on the office of Prime Minister, framed as a check on the 22-year incumbency of Tun Dr Mahathir Mohamad (1981–2003)
- **2019 Dec 3**: Minister in the Prime Minister's Department (Law) Datuk Liew Vui Keong tables the Constitution (Amendment) Bill 2019 in the Dewan Rakyat for first reading, proposing a two-term cap; second/third readings deferred to 2020
- **2020 Feb–Aug**: Sheraton Move collapses the PH government; Muhyiddin Yassin's Perikatan Nasional administration takes over
- **2020 Aug 26**: Minister in the PM's Department (Parliament and Law) Datuk Takiyuddin Hassan formally withdraws the 2019 Bill following a unanimous Cabinet decision under PN
- **2022 Nov 24**: Anwar Ibrahim sworn in as 10th Prime Minister, leading a unity government of PH, BN, GPS, GRS and other partners; institutional reform restated as a Madani priority
- **2025 Mar–Apr**: Government signals a third attempt at PM term limits within the Madani reform framework alongside Freedom of Information and political financing legislation
- **2026 Jan 5**: In his New Year address, PM Anwar Ibrahim announces the federal government will table a Constitution (Amendment) Bill to cap a Prime Minister's tenure at 10 years (two terms), describing it as a pillar of his institutional reform agenda
- **2026 Jan 12**: Malay Mail publishes a 7-question explainer on the proposal, confirming the cap would apply to aggregated time in office, continuous or otherwise, and would not affect former PMs who had already served 10 years
- **2026 Feb 20**: Anwar publicly urges quick passage of the two-term Bill ahead of the parliamentary sitting
- **2026 Feb 23**: Law Minister Datuk Seri Azalina Othman Said tables the Constitution (Amendment) Bill 2026 for first reading in the Dewan Rakyat; the Bill amends Article 43 of the Federal Constitution by inserting a new Clause (2A) (10-year aggregate cap) and Clauses (4A)–(4D) (cessation of office mechanism)
- **2026 Feb 27**: Bersih chairman Faisal Abdul Aziz publicly clarifies the amendment does not curtail the Yang di-Pertuan Agong's prerogative powers under Article 40, urging all MPs to support the Bill
- **2026 Mar 2**: Second reading commences; 41 MPs from government and opposition benches debate the Bill; Perikatan Nasional announces it will abstain
- **2026 Mar 2 (evening)**: Bloc vote held. 146 MPs vote in favour, 0 against, 44 abstain, 32 absent. Bill falls TWO votes short of the 148-vote two-thirds majority required under Article 159(3) for constitutional amendments. Speaker Tan Sri Johari Abdul declares the Bill not passed
- **2026 Mar 3**: All Pakatan Harapan MPs confirmed 100% present and voting in favour. Bersih 2.0 sharply criticises Perikatan Nasional's abstention as a setback for democratic accountability
- **2026 Mar 3**: Amirudin Shari (Selangor MB) calls the failed vote a reflection of Anwar's commitment to reform regardless of personal political cost
- **2026 Mar 4**: Communications Minister Datuk Fahmi Fadzil announces the Bill will be retabled in the June parliamentary sitting; FMT and BERNAMA report the eight government-bench absentees: Hishammuddin Hussein (Sembrong, UMNO), M Saravanan (Tapah, MIC), Suhaimi Nasir (Libaran), Larry Sng (Julau), Syed Abu Hussin Hafiz Syed Abdul Fasal (Bukit Gantang), Henry Sum Agong (Lawas, GPS), Riduan Rubin (Tenom, GRS) and Jeffrey Kitingan (Keningau, GRS-STAR)
- **2026 Mar 4**: Opposition Bersatu MP Azman Nasrudin (Padang Serai) tells reporters PN is "not rejecting the bill" but conditions support on a separate amendment barring the PM from concurrently holding the Finance portfolio — a direct reference to Anwar
- **2026 Mar 17**: FMT opinion column "PM term limit debate and the challenge of constitutional reform" frames the failure as evidence Malaysia's two-thirds threshold makes structural reform near-impossible without cross-bloc consensus
- **2026 Apr 6**: FMT analysts report delays in PN replacing Hamzah Zainudin as Opposition Leader, suggesting internal coalition strain that affected whip discipline during the March vote
- **2026 Apr 7**: Madani reform agenda — including Freedom of Information Bill and political financing law — under renewed scrutiny ahead of June retabling

## RELEVANT LAW

- **Federal Constitution, Article 43** — Establishes the Cabinet and the office of Prime Minister; Clause (2)(a) provides for the appointment of a member of the Dewan Rakyat who, in the YDPA's judgment, commands the confidence of the majority. The Bill proposed inserting Clause (2A) capping cumulative tenure at 10 years and Clauses (4A)–(4D) defining cessation mechanics
- **Federal Constitution, Article 159(3)** — Requires that any amendment to the Constitution (other than those listed in 159(4) and (5)) be passed by a majority of "not less than two-thirds of the total number of members" of each House at second and third readings. With 222 Dewan Rakyat seats, the threshold is 148 votes
- **Federal Constitution, Article 40** — Defines the YDPA's discretionary powers, including in the appointment of a Prime Minister; Bersih and the government argued the proposed cap does not curtail Article 40 because the YDPA's choice is still constrained only by who commands majority confidence
- **Federal Constitution, Article 38** — Conference of Rulers; not directly engaged by the proposed cap, since the office of PM is established under Article 43 and not subject to Rulers' consent for amendment
- **Standing Orders of the Dewan Rakyat** — Govern the bloc-vote procedure (undi belah bahagi) used on 2 March 2026
- **Constitution (Amendment) Bill 2019** — The withdrawn predecessor; legislative history relevant to the current attempt
- **Pakatan Harapan GE14 Manifesto (2018)** — Original political commitment to a two-term cap

## 12-DIMENSION RISK ASSESSMENT

1. **Sentiment** — MEDIUM: Public frustration with abstention politics; reform-minded urban voters disappointed but not hostile; rural and traditional voters largely indifferent
2. **Political** — HIGH: Direct test of unity government cohesion and opposition discipline; exposes Madani reform agenda's structural fragility against the two-thirds bar
3. **Ethnic** — LOW: Not ethnically framed; cuts across Malay, Chinese, Indian, Sabah/Sarawak Bumiputera lines; institutional process story
4. **Religious** — LOW: No religious dimension despite PAS involvement on the abstention bench
5. **Narrative** — CRITICAL: "Two votes short" — a self-imposed limit on executive power, voted yes by 146, no by zero, killed by absence and abstention. The story everyone tells (Anwar's reform failed) hides the structural story (no MP would say no on the record)
6. **Completeness** — HIGH: Coverage centres on the vote tally; thinner on what the eight government absentees signal about coalition discipline, and on whether PN's "conditional support" (no PM-cum-Finance Minister) is genuine reform or political targeting
7. **Temporal** — HIGH: Vote on 2 March 2026; retabling slated for June 2026; window for political realignment is short
8. **Confidence** — HIGH: Vote tally is public record; Bernama, FMT, Malay Mail, The Star, The Edge, Sinar Daily, Diplomat, ConstitutionNet all corroborate
9. **Sources** — HIGH: 22 sources spanning wire services, broadsheets, opinion analysis, civil society, international observers, Hansard equivalents
10. **Geographic** — MEDIUM: National story; Sabah/Sarawak coalition partners' attendance is consequential; absentee list spans Perak, Sabah, Sarawak, Johor
11. **Economic** — LOW–MEDIUM: Indirect effects via investor perception of Malaysia's institutional reform credibility; sovereign rating commentary possible
12. **Gender** — LOW: Not a gendered issue

## RECOMMENDED LENSES

**Governance** — The two-thirds amendment threshold and the politics of "abstention as veto"; how Madani's reform agenda collides with Article 159(3) arithmetic; coalition whip discipline under a unity government

**Political** — The gap between Anwar's reform brand and his coalition's whipping power; PN's strategic abstention as a politics of plausible deniability; the eight government absentees as a window on UMNO/GPS/GRS unease

**Historical** — Three attempts at the same reform (2018 manifesto, 2019 Bill withdrawn under PN, 2026 Bill defeated by abstention) and what the pattern reveals about Malaysian constitutional reform pathways; comparison with global term-limit precedents (US 22nd Amendment 1951, Philippines 1987 Constitution, Indonesia post-Suharto)
