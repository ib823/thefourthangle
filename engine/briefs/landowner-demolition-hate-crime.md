ISSUE: Landowner Rights to Demolish vs Religious Hate Crime Accusations — Malaysia's Unresolved Temple Land Crisis
PERIOD: February 2026 – ongoing (rooted in post-independence patterns since 1957)

CONTEXT:
- On 25 February 2026, the Sri Uchimalai Muniswaran Temple in Taman Rawang Perdana, Selangor was demolished in the early hours by the landowner, Yayasan Kubra, after deities were relocated. The temple had existed since 1995; Yayasan Kubra purchased the land in 2021.
- The "Tanah Malaya" group crowdfunded RM131,000 in one month — including RM45,000 for the backhoe used in the demolition — framing the act as enforcing landowner rights against illegal structures.
- Police opened investigations under Penal Code Sections 427 (mischief), 295 (injuring/defiling place of worship), 504 (intentional insult), and 447 (criminal trespass). Four men were arrested then released.
- On 13 March 2026, the Selayang Magistrates' Court barred further demolition, noting the temple had existed since 1995 and the site "has the potential to cause public tension."
- On 31 March 2026, the Shah Alam High Court set aside the magistrate's ruling, siding with the landowner. No grounds were provided. The temple committee filed an appeal.
- Separately, fugitive activist Tamim Dahri Abdul Razak (believed in Saudi Arabia) offered to surrender if authorities demolish four specific Hindu temples — escalating the crisis into explicit religious targeting.
- In 2025, the Dewi Sri Pathrakaliamman Temple (built 1894, 131 years old) in KL was ordered relocated to make way for Masjid Madani on land owned by Jakel Trading Sdn Bhd since 2012. The temple committee agreed to relocate 50 metres away.
- On the eve of Ponggal (13 January 2026), Jakel issued a 7-day eviction notice to the KL temple.
- PM Anwar Ibrahim stated the government will "no longer allow temples or shrines that do not comply with existing laws" and authorized municipal councils to clear such areas, while also saying "I cannot imagine myself as prime minister demolishing any temple."
- The 2018 Seafield Sri Maha Mariamman Temple riot — where One City Development's lawyers hired men to "take control" of the temple, triggering violence that killed firefighter Muhammad Adib Mohd Kassim — ended with zero convictions. All 17 men charged were acquitted in June 2023.

RELEVANT LAW:
- Federal Constitution Article 11: Freedom of religion — right to profess, practice, and propagate religion; subject to Art 11(5) public order, health, morality limits
- Federal Constitution Article 13: Right to property — no deprivation except in accordance with law; compulsory acquisition requires adequate compensation
- Federal Constitution Article 3: Islam as religion of the Federation; Art 3(4) states this does not affect other constitutional provisions
- National Land Code 1965 (Act 56): Section 48 — no title acquired by possession/unlawful occupation for any period; Section 341 — squatters have no rights in law or equity; no adverse possession doctrine in Malaysia (Federal Court: Sidek Bin Haji Muhamad v Government of Perak [1982])
- Penal Code Section 295: Injuring or defiling place of worship — up to 2 years imprisonment or fine or both
- Penal Code Section 298: Uttering words to wound religious feelings — up to 1 year imprisonment or fine or both
- Penal Code Section 298A: Causing disharmony on grounds of religion — 2 to 5 years imprisonment
- Penal Code Section 427: Mischief causing damage RM25+
- Penal Code Section 504: Intentional insult with intent to provoke breach of peace
- Penal Code Section 447: Criminal trespass
- Town and Country Planning Act 1976 (Act 172): Section 19 — no development without planning permission; Section 26 — offences for unauthorized development
- Street, Drainage and Building Act 1974 (Act 133): Section 72 — demolition/removal of unauthorized buildings (requires notice)
- Emergency (Clearance of Squatters) Regulations 1969: 7 days written notice before entering land and demolishing illegal structures

KEY STATISTICS:
- Estimated 3,200 Hindu temples in Malaysia; majority unregistered (MHS estimate: ~2,500 unregistered)
- Selangor alone: 773 temples, all reportedly built without formal permission
- ~10,000 Hindu temples (including estate shrines) demolished since independence 1957 (Hindu American Foundation estimate)
- Conservative estimate: 10,000 temples demolished since 1985
- HINDRAF documented 79 instances of demolition, demolition notices, or arson attacks between February 2006 and June 2007
- One temple demolished approximately every three weeks (HINDRAF lawyer estimate, 2007)
- Only Penang has a legally empowered Hindu endowments board; Pakatan Harapan pledged to establish boards in 6 other states — unfulfilled
- Malaysian Indians: ~7% of population (~2.3 million), 10.3% in Selangor (2020 Census)
- Tanah Malaya raised RM131,000 in one month; RM45,000 spent on backhoe
- Seafield temple riot (2018): 1 firefighter killed, 21 arrested, 17 charged, 0 convicted (all acquitted June 2023)
- Rawang temple: existed since 1995; landowner purchased 2021; midnight demolition 25 Feb 2026
- KL Dewi Sri temple: built 1894; land sold to Jakel 2012; relocation agreed 2025

12-DIMENSION RISK ASSESSMENT:
- Dim 1 (Sentiment): CRITICAL — temples are sacred spaces; demolition triggers deep emotional response across Hindu community and international diaspora
- Dim 2 (Political): HIGH — PM Anwar caught between Malay-Muslim majority expectations and Indian-Hindu minority support; opposition exploiting both sides
- Dim 3 (Ethnic): CRITICAL — issue is structurally ethnic (Indian-origin temples on Malay-owned land); T4A must focus ONLY on legal/policy frameworks, never target communities
- Dim 4 (Religious): CRITICAL — direct conflict between Islam-aligned land use and Hindu worship rights; 3R sensitivity at maximum; analysis must critique policy and law, not beliefs or communities
- Dim 5 (Narrative): HIGH — dominant narrative frames this as "illegal structures" vs "religious hate crime" binary; both frames are reductive
- Dim 6 (Completeness): HIGH — the colonial-era root cause (plantation workers had no land rights) is routinely omitted; registration gap is structural, not individual failure
- Dim 7 (Timing): CURRENT — High Court ruling 31 March 2026; appeal pending; policy still evolving
- Dim 8 (Certainty): MEDIUM — temple count estimates vary widely (2,500 to 17,000 unregistered); demolition statistics are advocacy-sourced, not independently verified
- Dim 9 (Source diversity): GOOD — FMT, Malay Mail, SCMP, Malaysiakini, The Star, legal databases, USCIRF, MHS, HINDRAF records
- Dim 10 (Geographic): MEDIUM — concentrated in Selangor/KL but national implications; Penang exception (has Hindu endowments board)
- Dim 11 (Economic): HIGH — land values drive demolition incentives; developers profit from clearing temple sites; B40 Indian communities bear costs
- Dim 12 (Gender): LOW — gender is not a primary axis in this issue, though women devotees and temple committees are affected
