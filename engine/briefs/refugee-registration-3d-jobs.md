# ISSUE 1966 — Refugee Registration Launches — But Only "3D" Jobs Allowed

## PERIOD
January–April 2026

## CONTEXT (Timeline)
- 2023: National Security Council directive mandates creation of refugee registration system
- Jul 2025: Ministry of Home Affairs begins piloting DPP database through Immigration Department
- Jan 1, 2026: Dokumen Pendaftaran Pelarian (DPP) — Malaysia's first national refugee registration system — officially launches
- Jan 2026: 37,000+ individuals screened to date
- Feb 21, 2026: FMT opinion piece outlines "factors in favour" of refugee registration
- Mar 2026: Government confirms work permits restricted to "3D" jobs (dirty, dangerous, difficult) — manufacturing, mining, construction
- Ongoing: UNHCR continues parallel registration; 211,260 refugees and asylum-seekers on its rolls
- Ongoing: 18,000 migrants, refugees, asylum seekers held in immigration detention centers
- Ongoing: Economists estimate legal refugee employment could add RM3 billion to GDP

## ACTORS
- Ministry of Home Affairs — Administers DPP system
- Immigration Department — Pilot screening and registration
- National Security Council — Issued original directive
- UNHCR Malaysia — Parallel registration of 211,260 refugees; supportive but cautious
- Human Resources Ministry — Considering formal employment for refugees in "3D" sectors
- Rohingya community — 124,123 individuals, largest refugee group
- Myanmar refugees (non-Rohingya) — Second largest group
- Civil society (Asylum Access, Refugee Malaysia) — Advocacy for broader work rights
- Malaysian employers — Currently use informal refugee labor; formal system would regulate
- Center for Global Development — Research on economic benefits of refugee work rights

## RELEVANT LAW
- Immigration Act 1959/63 — Refugees have no legal status; subject to arrest and detention
- Anti-Trafficking in Persons and Anti-Smuggling of Migrants Act 2007
- Employment Act 1955 — Does not extend protections to undocumented workers
- 1951 Refugee Convention — Malaysia is NOT a signatory
- Federal Constitution Article 6 — Prohibition of slavery and forced labour
- National Security Council directive (2023) — Mandate for DPP creation

## KEY STATISTICS
- 211,260 — Refugees and asylum-seekers registered with UNHCR in Malaysia (May 2025)
- 124,123 — Rohingya refugees from Myanmar (largest group)
- 37,000+ — Individuals screened under new DPP system
- 18,000 — Migrants/refugees held in immigration detention centers
- 800,000+ — Bangladeshi workers with Malaysian work permits (largest documented foreign worker group)
- RM3 billion — Estimated annual GDP contribution if refugees allowed legal employment
- RM50 million — Estimated annual tax revenue from legal refugee employment
- 50+ — Countries of origin represented among Malaysia's refugee population
- 0 — Number of years Malaysia has been signatory to 1951 Refugee Convention

## 12-DIMENSION RISK ASSESSMENT
1. **Sentiment** — HIGH: Public opinion divided; xenophobic sentiment strong on social media
2. **Political** — HIGH: Registration seen as legitimizing refugees; sensitive with Malay-majority electorate
3. **Ethnic** — HIGH: Rohingya face particular discrimination; racial tensions with local communities
4. **Religious** — MEDIUM: Muslim Rohingya vs Buddhist Myanmar context; solidarity vs resource competition
5. **Narrative** — CRITICAL: "Humanitarian milestone" vs "legitimizing illegal immigrants" — deeply polarized framing
6. **Completeness** — HIGH: UNHCR data, CGD research, government statements, activist reports
7. **Temporal** — MEDIUM: System launched Jan 2026; work permit scope still being defined
8. **Confidence** — HIGH: UNHCR official data, government announcements, CGD research
9. **Sources** — HIGH: UNHCR, CGD, HRW, Asylum Access, FMT, Malay Mail, SCMP
10. **Geographic** — HIGH: Concentrated in KL, Selangor, Penang; detention centers nationwide
11. **Economic** — HIGH: RM3B GDP potential vs "taking local jobs" perception
12. **Gender** — MEDIUM: Refugee women face additional exploitation; domestic work not in "3D" category
