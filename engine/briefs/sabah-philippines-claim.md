ISSUE: The Sabah Claim That Won't Die — What the Philippines Wants, What Malaysia Won, and What Nobody Settled
PERIOD: February 2026 (NAMRIA map trigger) — ongoing. Historical roots: 1878–present.

CONTEXT:
- In February 2026, the Philippines' National Mapping and Resource Information Authority (NAMRIA) released an updated administrative map depicting Sabah as Philippine territory, primarily intended to assert sovereignty in the West Philippine Sea but reigniting the dormant Sabah claim.
- On 20 February 2026, DFA maritime affairs spokesperson Rogelio Villanueva formally reaffirmed the Philippine government's sovereignty claim over Sabah at a media briefing in Pasay City.
- The Philippines enacted the Philippine Maritime Zones Act of 2024 (Republic Act 12064), which did NOT repeal Section 2 of Republic Act 5446 — the 1968 law that includes Sabah within Philippines' claimed baselines.
- Malaysia's Foreign Minister Datuk Seri Mohamad Hasan formally protested, saying the Philippine legislation encroaches upon Malaysia's maritime boundaries off Sabah's coast.
- Sabah PKR slammed the "fresh call" and reaffirmed Sabah's sovereignty within Malaysia (FMT, 27 March 2026).

HISTORICAL FOUNDATION:
- 22 January 1878: Sultan Jamal ul-Azam of Sulu signed an agreement with British merchants Gustavus Baron von Overbeck and Alfred Dent, transferring North Borneo (Sabah) to the British North Borneo Company.
- THE CORE DISPUTE: The English text says "ceded" (permanent transfer). The Malay/Sulu text uses "pajakkan" — interpreted by the Philippines as "lease" (temporary, revocable). This single word underpins 148 years of dispute.
- 1881: British North Borneo Chartered Company granted royal charter. Administered Sabah until 1946.
- 1946: British Crown Colony of North Borneo established after WWII.
- 1962: Cobbold Commission surveyed opinion in North Borneo — concluded roughly two-thirds supported joining Malaysia (one-third fully, one-third with safeguards).
- 16 September 1963: Malaysia formed. Sabah joined as a founding state under the Malaysia Agreement (MA63).
- 1963: Philippines broke diplomatic ties with Malaysia over Sabah. Ties restored 1966.
- 1968: Republic Act 5446 passed — Philippines defines its baselines to include Sabah.
- 1963–2013: Malaysia paid annual "cession money" of RM5,300 (approx) to Sulu heirs as acknowledgment of the 1878 agreement.
- February 2013: Lahad Datu standoff — armed followers of Sulu claimant Jamalul Kiram III invaded eastern Sabah. Malaysian security forces repelled them. 68 killed. Malaysia stopped annual payments.
- 2019: Eight self-proclaimed heirs of Sultanate of Sulu initiated arbitration in Madrid against Malaysia, seeking billions in compensation.
- February 2022: Spanish arbitrator issued interim award of US$14.92 billion against Malaysia (in absentia — Malaysia refused to participate, disputing jurisdiction).
- June 2023: The Hague Court of Appeal dismissed the Sulu heirs' attempt to enforce the award. Found Malaysia was not properly served and the arbitration was flawed.
- September 2024: Supreme Court of the Netherlands upheld the Hague dismissal.
- November 2024: France's Cour de Cassation dismissed the heirs' appeal.
- 9 December 2025: Paris Court of Appeal ANNULLED the US$14.92 billion final award entirely. Found the arbitration agreement in the 1878 document was "inapplicable" because the reference to the British Consul General — a key element of the parties' consent — no longer exists. Ordered claimants to pay €200,000 to Malaysia.

KEY QUESTIONS (T4A ANGLE):
1. Why does the Philippines STILL claim Sabah despite losing every court case?
   → Because RA5446 (1968) has never been repealed. It's enshrined in Philippine law. Every Philippine president who drops the claim risks being accused of "giving away territory."
2. Why did Malaysia WIN in every international court?
   → Three reasons: (a) the 1878 agreement's English text says "ceded" not "leased"; (b) the Sulu sultanate no longer exists as a sovereign entity; (c) the 1962 Cobbold Commission and 1963 MA63 constituted self-determination under international law.
3. What hasn't been settled?
   → The ICJ has NEVER ruled on the merits. Malaysia refuses to submit. The Philippines can't force it. All court victories were about arbitration procedure, not the underlying sovereignty question. The foundational "ceded vs leased" question remains legally unresolved by any court of competent jurisdiction.
4. What does Sabah itself think?
   → Sabah's own political class (across parties) unanimously rejects the Philippine claim. But Sabah also has unresolved grievances with Kuala Lumpur under MA63 — special grant frozen at RM26.7M since 1969, revenue-sharing disputes, development gaps. The external claim obscures the internal conversation.
5. What is the security dimension?
   → The 2013 Lahad Datu incursion killed 68 people. ESSZONE (Eastern Sabah Security Zone) was created. Cross-border movement between Sulu and eastern Sabah remains porous. The Philippines' claim is not merely diplomatic — it has manifested in armed incursion within living memory.
