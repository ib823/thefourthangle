# ISSUE 1973 — Bestinet: The RM400M Migrant Visa Monopoly Everyone Protects

## PERIOD
January 2024 – April 2026

## CONTEXT (Timeline)

- **2008**: Aminul Islam Abdul Nor (known as "Amin"), a Bangladeshi-born Malaysian citizen, founds Bestinet Sdn Bhd, developing software to digitize Malaysia's paper-based migrant worker recruitment system. (Bloomberg, Jan 23 2026)
- **2014 (March 24)**: Bestinet registers a subsidiary in Bangladesh — Bestinet Bangladesh Limited — with Aminul Islam as Chairman and Ruhul Amin as Managing Director. In 2022, the company name is changed to Catharsis Solutions Limited. (Prothom Alo, Sep 20 2024)
- **2015**: Malaysia adopts Bestinet's Foreign Workers Centralised Management System (FWCMS) while Ahmad Zahid Hamidi is Home Affairs Minister. Zahid announces Malaysia will bring in up to 1.5 million Bangladeshi workers. Board includes former Home Minister Azmi Khalid and former Labour Department Director-General Tengku Omar Tengku Bot. (Bloomberg, Jan 2026; C4 Center, Jul 2024)
- **2015**: All recruitment orders from Bangladesh restricted to just 10 of Bangladesh's 1,000+ licensed agencies, ending open competition. Former Bangladeshi officials later say Malaysia insisted on this arrangement, threatening to recruit workers from elsewhere if Bangladesh did not comply. (Bloomberg, Jan 2026)
- **2015 (January)**: Immigration Department suspends Bestinet's biometric health checks and visa services following complaints, but the suspension is short-lived. (Malay Mail, Jan 30 2015)
- **2018 (January 12)**: Home Ministry issues Letter of Acceptance for FWCMS development, but no formal contract is signed for nearly six years. (PAC Report, Jul 2024)
- **2018**: Agencies excluded from the 10-agency list sue the selected firms and Bangladesh's government, calling the arrangement a cartel that was "not selected on merit" and alleging Amin helped design the scheme. (Bloomberg, Jan 2026)
- **2022 (July)**: MACC raids Bestinet for alleged corruption and abuse of power involving the selection of 25 Bangladeshi recruitment agencies. (C4 Center; MalaysiaNow)
- **2024 (early)**: A memo prepared for PM Anwar Ibrahim by the Madani Research Centre documents that per-person recruitment costs have reached US$6,600 — more than double what Bangladeshis paid before Bestinet's involvement and more than double what Indonesians and Nepalis pay. (Bloomberg, Jan 2026)
- **2024 (May 29)**: Cabinet agrees to extend Bestinet's FWCMS contract. Home Minister Saifuddin Nasution Ismail announces a three-year extension despite there being no signed contract to begin with. (Andy Hall/Malaysiakini, Jun 1 2024; C4 Center)
- **2024 (May 31)**: Malaysia halts all new Bangladeshi migrant worker recruitment after widespread reports of job scams where workers paid thousands of dollars for jobs that never existed. (Bloomberg, May 2025; SCMP)
- **2024 (May-June)**: The Daily Star reports syndicate siphoned over US$1 billion out of Bangladesh in recruitment fees from August 2022 to April 2024, covering approximately 450,000 workers. 453 Bangladeshi recruitment agents sign letter alleging Ruhul Amin Swapan (Catharsis International) collected these syndicate fees. (Daily Star, May 31 2024)
- **2024 (July 3)**: PAC chastises government for running FWCMS for nearly six years without a contract, calling it a governance violation. PAC finds system had 24 unauthorized users who approved employer applications. (PAC; The Star; The Edge)
- **2024 (August 14)**: Cabinet approves the formal FWCMS contract.
- **2024 (September 3)**: Contract finalized — runs retroactively from June 1, 2024 to January 31, 2031 (6+ years). Fee per foreign worker permit more than doubled from RM100 to RM215, exceeding the RM86 rate approved by the PM's Public-Private Partnership Unit and the RM120 that Bestinet itself originally requested. (PAC; The Edge)
- **2024 (September)**: MACC Chief Azam Baki declares the Bestinet probe "NFA" (No Further Action), saying "no evidence of criminal activity" was found, despite the July 2022 raids. This occurs shortly after the lucrative contract extension. (Bestinet.com.my; MACC)
- **2024 (September 20)**: Prothom Alo reports Aminul Islam and Ruhul Amin Swapan involved in alleged migrant worker syndicate money laundering from Bangladesh to Malaysia. (Prothom Alo)
- **2024 (October 24)**: Interpol's National Central Bureau (NCB) in Bangladesh writes to NCB Malaysia, requesting arrest and extradition of Aminul Islam and Ruhul Amin Swapan for investigation into trafficking and extortion. (Bloomberg, Nov 2024; Daily Star; Malay Mail)
- **2024 (November 6)**: Home Minister Saifuddin confirms Bangladesh's extradition request is being managed on a government-to-government and police-to-police basis, but adds Malaysia will only extradite if Dhaka files formal charges. Aminul denies all misconduct. (Malay Mail; FMT; The Star)
- **2024 (November 27)**: MalaysiaNow reports PAC findings showing the contract extension could cost government at least RM3.2 billion, raising questions about a "windfall worth billions for company linked to man wanted by Bangladesh." (MalaysiaNow)
- **2025 (February – December)**: FWCMS issues 2,353,297 Temporary Work Visit Passes, generating RM381.3 million in fees paid to Bestinet. (The Edge, Feb 2026; NST)
- **2025 (April)**: Andy Hall reports Nepali workers forced to pay RM10,000 to secure security guard jobs in Malaysia, extending syndicate model beyond Bangladesh. (FMT, Apr 20 2025)
- **2025 (May 15)**: Bloomberg exclusive: Malaysia officially asks Bangladesh to withdraw "unsubstantiated allegations" of labor trafficking to improve Malaysia's US TIP Report rating. Malaysia's Human Resources Ministry Secretary-General writes letter stating allegations "have impacted the Malaysian reputation." (Bloomberg, May 15 2025)
- **2025 (May 17)**: 33 Bangladeshi workers file lawsuit in Shah Alam High Court against Meranti Binamas Sdn Bhd and the Malaysian government, seeking RM1.72 million, alleging they were victims of human trafficking and a job scam after each paying RM25,000 in fees. (FMT; Daily Star)
- **2025 (May 23)**: Malaysia announces reopening of labor market to Bangladeshi migrants, but the syndicate system stays. (TBS, May 23 2025)
- **2025 (July 31)**: Malaysiakini reports Bangladesh agreed to stop its investigation into the exploitation syndicate, following meetings between Bangladeshi and Malaysian officials in Kuala Lumpur. (Malaysiakini)
- **2025 (November 6)**: New Malaysian migrant worker "10 mandatory criteria" spark scandal in Nepal, with at least five agencies paying RM1–1.5 million each to intermediaries claiming ties to Malaysian officials who control quotas. Syndicate model replicates. (Malaysiakini, Nov 6 2025)
- **2025 (November 21)**: UN OHCHR experts sound alarm over "continued systematic exploitation" of Bangladeshi migrant workers in Malaysia, citing thousands stranded after paying recruitment fees five times higher than official rates. (OHCHR press release)
- **2025 (November 26)**: Human Rights Watch publishes "Rampant Labor Abuses Against Bangladeshi Migrant Workers in Malaysia," documenting debt bondage, passport confiscation, false job promises, and a closed syndicate sustained by corruption. (HRW)
- **2025 (November-December)**: Bangladesh Expatriate Welfare Adviser announces Malaysia may recruit up to 40,000 Bangladeshi workers in 2026, far below the speculated 1–1.2 million. (Staffing Industry Analysts; Daily Star)
- **2025 (November 18 – 2026 March 11)**: Bestinet's CFO Kunal appointed as non-independent non-executive director of NexG Bhd — the company holding exclusive contracts for Malaysian passport booklets, MyKad identity cards, and foreign worker identification cards. Creates conflict-of-interest concerns. (The Edge, Mar 2026)
- **2026 (January 23)**: Bloomberg publishes landmark 10-month investigation: "Everyone Gets a Cut, and Migrant Workers Pay the Price." Key finding: Companies founded by Amin pocketed more than US$100 million (RM400 million) in 10 years. Six sources say Amin selected the agencies while Zahid handled the politics. (Bloomberg)
- **2026 (January 26)**: MalaysiaNow publishes analysis: "Damning Bloomberg report implicates Putrajaya in giving second life to Bangladeshi-owned firm accused of human trafficking." (MalaysiaNow)
- **2026 (February)**: Bestinet and Aminul Islam file libel suit against 10 media outlets and individuals — including Bloomberg, Malaysiakini, The Edge, MalaysiaNow, Rafizi Ramli, and Latheefa Koya — seeking over RM1 billion in damages. (BFM; The Edge; NST)
- **2026 (February)**: Rafizi Ramli reveals government has no access to Bestinet's FWCMS source code, meaning the government is locked out of its own migrant worker system. (Malaysiakini; MalaysiaNow)
- **2026 (February 23)**: KL High Court rejects Bestinet's bid for gag order/interim injunction against Bloomberg and 8 others, ruling that granting the injunction would prevent wrongdoing from being discussed publicly. (MalaysiaNow; NST; The Edge)
- **2026 (April 2)**: Bloomberg Malaysia files application to strike out the libel suit entirely. Hearing set for June 26. (FMT; The Vibes; The Edge)

## RELEVANT LAW

1. **Anti-Trafficking in Persons and Anti-Smuggling of Migrants Act 2007 (ATIPSOM)** — Criminalizes labor trafficking; 3-20 years imprisonment. Key gap: officials conflate trafficking with smuggling.
2. **Employment Act 1955 (Amended)** — Criminalizes forced labor; up to 2 years and RM100,000 fine. Weaker than ATIPSOM.
3. **Immigration Act 1959/63** — Criminalizes irregular entry; used to punish workers who become undocumented due to employer/recruiter fraud.
4. **MACC Act 2009** — Relevant to NFA decision on Bestinet probe.
5. **Defamation Act 1957** — Basis for Bestinet's RM1 billion libel suit.
6. **ILO Convention C29 (Forced Labour)** — Malaysia ratified; relevant to debt bondage findings.
7. **ILO Convention C181 (Private Employment Agencies)** — Not ratified by Malaysia.
8. **ILO Fair Recruitment Initiative** — Workers should not pay recruitment fees.
9. **Malaysia-Bangladesh MoU on Migrant Workers** — Official cost ~US$750; actual cost 5-8x higher.
10. **US Trafficking Victims Protection Act** — Basis for annual TIP Report; Malaysia Tier 2.
11. **Mutual termination clause in FWCMS contract** — Government financially liable if it terminates unilaterally.
