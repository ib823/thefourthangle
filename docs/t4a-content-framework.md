# FOUNDATIONAL PRINCIPLE

T4A is neutral in analysis but NOT neutral in presentation energy.

Neutral ≠ boring. Neutral = no political lean.
The content must be ALIVE, URGENT, COMPELLING — while taking no side.

The Endgame Principle:
> The title is created LAST, after the full analysis is complete.
> It must reveal nothing of the verdict.
> It must reveal everything about the stakes.
> It must make NOT clicking feel like a loss.

This principle applies to every content element in this framework.

---

# OPERATIONAL CONFIDENTIALITY RULE

This framework describes what the reader SEES. It must never reveal what PRODUCES the content. All user-facing text must comply with addendum-no-ai-references.md. Specifically:

- The pipeline has 6 stages. Publicly they are called: Primary Analysis, Bias Audit, Fact Verification, Alternative Framing, Contrarian Stress-Test, Synthesis Review.
- The technology powering each stage is proprietary and confidential.
- No user-facing element may reference AI, language models, specific providers, or any proprietary tooling.
- The methodology (math, dimensions, scoring) IS public. The tooling is NOT.

---

# CREATION SEQUENCING (critical — defines the entire pipeline)

Content is NOT written top-down. It is written inside-out.

```
STEP 1:  Full 6-stage adversarial pipeline completes (the truth is established)
STEP 2:  Synthesis review produces the unified analysis
STEP 3:  Bias vectors, scores, omissions are calculated (12 dimensions)
STEP 4:  Article body is composed from synthesis
STEP 5:  Section headers are written to frame the body
STEP 6:  Visual concept is generated (what image/sketch fits?)
STEP 7:  Card summary is distilled from the full article
STEP 8:  Card headline is crafted LAST — the Endgame moment

           ┌─────────────────────────────────────┐
           │                                     │
           │   HEADLINE (written last)           │  ← Step 8
           │        ↑                            │
           │   SUMMARY (distilled)               │  ← Step 7
           │        ↑                            │
           │   VISUAL (conceptualised)           │  ← Step 6
           │        ↑                            │
           │   SECTION HEADERS (framing)         │  ← Step 5
           │        ↑                            │
           │   ARTICLE BODY (composed)           │  ← Step 4
           │        ↑                            │
           │   SCORES & VECTORS (calculated)     │  ← Step 3
           │        ↑                            │
           │   SYNTHESIS (unified truth)         │  ← Step 2
           │        ↑                            │
           │   RAW 6-STAGE ANALYSIS (foundation) │  ← Step 1
           │                                     │
           └─────────────────────────────────────┘
```

Why this order matters: You cannot write a headline that is both
exciting AND non-contradictory unless you know EVERYTHING first.
A headline written before analysis is either misleading or bland.
A headline written after analysis is precise AND electric.

## 1A. CATEGORY BADGE + TIME STAMP

**Purpose:** Instant context. Reader knows the domain and freshness
before their eyes even reach the headline.

**Format:**
```
POLITICS · 23 min ago
ECONOMY · 2 hours ago
BREAKING · just now          ← red background, white text
```

**Word rules:**
- Category: ONE word only. From fixed set:
  POLITICS | ECONOMY | SOCIAL | RIGHTS | ENVIRONMENT | SECURITY | GOVERNANCE
- Time: relative, human ("23 min ago" not "2026-03-23T14:22:00Z")
- BREAKING: reserved for analyses published within 60 minutes of the
  source event. Never used for routine coverage. If overused, it loses power.
  Maximum: 2 BREAKING badges visible in feed at any time.

**Why these specific categories:**
These seven categories map to the areas where Malaysian public
discourse is most active and most vulnerable to partisan framing.
They are broad enough to cover everything, specific enough to
signal what kind of stakes are involved.

## 1B. THE VISUAL

**Purpose:** Emotional entry point. The visual bypasses the analytical
brain and creates a FEELING before the headline creates a THOUGHT.

**What it is NOT:**
- Not a news photo (T4A doesn't do photojournalism)
- Not realistic imagery (uncanny, untrustworthy)
- Not a stock illustration (generic, forgettable)

**What it IS:**
A purpose-built conceptual sketch — minimal, symbolic, hand-drawn aesthetic.
Think editorial illustration in The Economist or courtroom sketches.
Bold lines. Limited palette (2-3 colours from T4A brand palette).
Abstract enough to not editorialise. Concrete enough to be recognisable.

**Visual styles available (the pipeline selects the appropriate one):**

```
STYLE 1: SYMBOLIC
Single iconic object that represents the core tension.
Example: A policy on digital economy →
  sketch of a padlock with a circuit board pattern.
  Says "digital + control" without saying "good" or "bad."

STYLE 2: TENSION PAIR
Two objects or symbols in visual opposition.
Example: Budget reallocation from education to defence →
  a book and a shield on opposite ends of a seesaw.
  Shows the tradeoff. Takes no side on which matters more.

STYLE 3: SCALE/MAGNITUDE
A visual that communicates the SIZE of the issue.
Example: RM50 billion infrastructure plan →
  tiny human figure next to enormous geometric blocks.
  Communicates scale without commenting on merit.

STYLE 4: PROCESS/FLOW
A simple diagram showing movement or change.
Example: New immigration policy →
  arrows flowing through a funnel that narrows.
  Shows "restriction" without labelling it good or bad.

STYLE 5: ABSENCE
What's NOT shown is the point.
Example: Missing accountability in a scandal →
  an empty chair at a table with documents.
  The absence IS the statement.
```

**Visual generation rules:**
- Generated at Step 6, AFTER full analysis is complete
- Must not favour any political reading of the issue
- Must not include real faces, flags, or party symbols
- Must use only T4A brand colours (red, blue, slate, silver on white/dark)
- Must work at 80×80px (card thumbnail) AND 400×300px (featured)
- Style is selected by the synthesis review based on which visual
  metaphor best represents the STRUCTURE of the issue, not its conclusion

**Dimensions:**
- Card: 80×80px square, right-aligned or top-aligned depending on layout
- Featured card: full-width, 16:9 ratio, 400×225px minimum
- Article header: full-width, 3:1 ratio

## 1C. THE HEADLINE

**Purpose:** The intellectual hook. Makes the reader feel "I need to
know more about this" without telling them what to think.

**This is the hardest content element in T4A.** It must be:
- Exciting (creates urgency or curiosity)
- Neutral (does not telegraph the verdict)
- Accurate (does not distort the issue)
- Compact (strict character limits)
- Non-contradictory (cannot be proven wrong by the analysis below)

**The Endgame Test — every headline must pass ALL five:**

```
TEST 1: STAKES TEST
  "Does this headline make clear WHY this matters?"
  ✗ "New Policy Announced"           ← who cares?
  ✓ "RM50B Plan Reshapes Digital Economy" ← stakes clear

TEST 2: NEUTRALITY TEST
  "Could a reader from any political position click without
  feeling the headline already judged them?"
  ✗ "Government Fails on Promise"    ← verdict baked in
  ✗ "Bold New Vision for Malaysia"   ← verdict baked in
  ✓ "Promise Meets Reality: 5 Claims Checked" ← neutral frame

TEST 3: CURIOSITY GAP TEST
  "Does the headline open a question the reader needs answered?"
  ✗ "Analysis of Budget Allocation"  ← no question opened
  ✓ "Where Did the RM12B Go?"       ← question demands answer

TEST 4: SPOILER TEST (The Endgame Rule)
  "Does the headline reveal the analysis conclusion?"
  ✗ "Study Proves Opposition Wrong"  ← conclusion given away
  ✓ "Both Sides Cite Same Data, Reach Opposite Conclusions"
    ← intriguing, conclusion unknown

TEST 5: CONTRADICTION TEST
  "If the reader reads the full analysis, will the headline
  feel dishonest or misleading in retrospect?"
  ✗ "Shocking Revelation in Policy"  ← if analysis is mundane, trust breaks
  ✓ "The Number Both Sides Don't Mention" ← verifiable in analysis
```

**Word count:** 6–12 words. Never fewer, never more.

**Word selection principles:**

```
USE                          AVOID
─────────────────────────    ─────────────────────────
Concrete nouns               Abstract nouns
(RM50B, policy, data)        (situation, issue, matter)

Active verbs                 Passive constructions
(reshapes, reveals, splits)  (was announced, is being)

Numbers                      Vague quantities
(5 claims, RM12B, 6 stages)  (several, many, some)

Tension words                Judgment words
(versus, despite, but)       (fails, wins, best, worst)

Question frames              Statement frames
(where, how, what happens)   (this is, experts say)

Specific Malaysian context   Generic phrasing
(Dewan Rakyat, Budget 2026)  (parliament, the budget)
```

**Headline formulas that work for neutral analysis:**

```
FORMULA 1: [NUMBER] + [SPECIFIC THING] + [TENSION VERB]
"5 Budget Claims That Don't Add Up"
"3 Ministries, 3 Different Numbers"

FORMULA 2: [QUESTION WORD] + [SPECIFIC DETAIL]
"Where RM8B in Allocations Actually Went"
"What Both Coalitions Left Out of the Debate"

FORMULA 3: [X] vs [Y] + [NEUTRAL FRAME]
"State vs Federal: Same Data, Different Stories"
"Promise vs Delivery: 2024 Pledges Revisited"

FORMULA 4: THE [THING] + [NOBODY/EVERYONE] + [ACTION]
"The Clause Nobody Mentioned in the Amendment"
"The Number Every Minister Quoted Differently"

FORMULA 5: [BEHIND/INSIDE/BENEATH] + [THE VISIBLE EVENT]
"Behind the Unanimous Vote: What the Debate Revealed"
"Inside the Policy Reversal: A Timeline"
```

**Character limit:** 50 characters (card), 80 characters (featured/article)

## 1D. THE SUMMARY

**Purpose:** The promise of value. Tells the reader WHAT THEY WILL GAIN
by spending 5 minutes reading the full analysis. This is a transaction —
the summary must make the reader feel the exchange is worth it.

**The summary answers exactly one question:**
"What will I understand after reading this that I don't understand now?"

**Structure (always this structure, no exceptions):**

```
[SENTENCE 1: The situation in one line — what happened or what exists]
[SENTENCE 2: The tension — what makes this complicated or contested]
[SENTENCE 3: The T4A value-add — what the 6-stage analysis found]
```

**Example:**

```
Headline: "Where RM8B in Allocations Actually Went"

Summary:
"Budget 2026 allocated RM8B to digital infrastructure
across four ministries. Each ministry reports different
spending figures. Our 6-stage adversarial review traces every ringgit."

Sentence 1: RM8B allocated (the fact)
Sentence 2: Different figures reported (the tension)
Sentence 3: 6-stage review traces it (the T4A promise)
```

**Another example:**

```
Headline: "The Clause Nobody Mentioned in the Amendment"

Summary:
"Parliament passed the Communications Amendment with
bipartisan support last Thursday. Buried in Section 14A
is a provision that redefines platform liability. Six
independent review stages reached split conclusions."

Sentence 1: Amendment passed (the fact)
Sentence 2: Hidden clause redefines liability (the tension)
Sentence 3: Six review stages split on implications (the T4A promise)
```

**Approved phrasings for Sentence 3 (the T4A promise):**
```
"Our 6-stage adversarial review found..."
"Six independent editorial stages [agree/disagree/reveal]..."
"The multi-stage verdict..."
"Our adversarial editorial pipeline reveals..."
"Six review stages reached [split/unanimous/surprising] conclusions."
```

**NEVER use in Sentence 3:**
```
"Five AI models..."
"Our models found..."
"Multiple language models..."
Any reference to specific technology providers.
```

**Word count:** 25–42 words. Three sentences.

**Word selection rules for summaries:**
```
SENTENCE 1 (the fact):
  - Lead with the most concrete noun possible
  - Include a number, date, or proper noun
  - Maximum 15 words
  - Tone: reportorial, BBC-flat

SENTENCE 2 (the tension):
  - Must contain a contrast word: "but," "however," "yet,"
    "despite," "while," "although," "different," "disputed"
  - This is where energy lives — the tension word creates
    the gap between what seems true and what might be true
  - Maximum 15 words
  - Tone: investigative, leaning forward

SENTENCE 3 (the T4A promise):
  - Must reference the multi-stage editorial process
  - Use ONLY approved phrasings (see above)
  - This sentence is WHY they click — it promises
    something they can't get anywhere else
  - Maximum 12 words
  - Tone: authoritative, confident
```

**The summary is written at Step 7 — AFTER the article body exists.**
It is a distillation, not a preview. It must be perfectly consistent
with the full analysis. No word in the summary can be contradicted
by the article.

## 1E. THE VERDICT BAR

**Purpose:** Instant credibility signal. Before reading a single word
of analysis, the reader sees that multiple independent editorial stages
assessed this issue and can see at a glance whether they agree.

**What it shows:**

```
🟢🟡🟢🟢🟡🟢  Neutrality: 78/100
PA  BA  FC  AF  CT  SR
```

**Components:**

```
STAGE DOTS:
  6 circles, one per editorial stage, always in this order:
  PA (Primary Analysis) → BA (Bias Audit) → FC (Fact Check) →
  AF (Alt Framing) → CT (Contrarian Test) → SR (Synthesis Review)

  Colours:
  🟢 GREEN  = stage rates this analysis as balanced (score ≥ 75)
  🟡 YELLOW = stage found moderate concerns (score 50-74)
  🔴 RED    = stage found significant issues (score < 50)

  The ORDER never changes. Reader learns to recognise patterns.
  "PA and FC agree, BA flagged something" becomes readable at a glance.

NEUTRALITY SCORE:
  Single number out of 100.
  This is the composite audit score from the mathematical framework
  (geometric median distance + spectral scores + conflict + completeness).
  Displayed as: "78/100" in monospace font.

SCORE BAR:
  Visual bar, filled proportionally.
  Gradient: red (0) → yellow (50) → blue (100)
  NOT green for high scores — green implies "good."
  Blue implies "analytical depth" — T4A's brand colour for trust.
```

**Per-stage dot scoring:**
```
PA dot: Based on the initial analysis quality score from synthesis
BA dot: Based on bias_score from Stage 2 output
FC dot: Based on factual_accuracy_score from Stage 3 output
AF dot: Based on completeness_score from Stage 4 output
CT dot: Based on courage_score from Stage 5 output
SR dot: Based on final composite audit score
```

**Why show this on the card (not just the article):**
The verdict bar is T4A's signature. It is the visual proof that this
is not opinion journalism. Every card carries the evidence of process.
It differentiates T4A from every other news source in the feed.

**Word count:** Zero prose words. Pure data visualisation.
Labels only: stage abbreviations (2 chars each) + score (max 3 digits).

## 3B. THE ANALYSIS (article body)

**Structure — always five sections, always this order:**

```
SECTION 1: WHAT HAPPENED
  Purpose: Establish the undisputed facts
  Tone: Wire-service flat. Reuters energy.
  Word count: 100–150 words
  Contains: dates, names, numbers, direct actions
  Does NOT contain: interpretation, framing, context

  Why this first: Reader must have the raw facts before
  any analysis. This section is the shared foundation
  that every stage's analysis builds upon.

SECTION 2: WHY IT MATTERS
  Purpose: Provide the context that makes facts meaningful
  Tone: Analytical. The Economist energy.
  Word count: 150–200 words
  Contains: historical context, precedents, affected populations,
            economic/social/legal implications
  Does NOT contain: predictions, opinions, partisan framing

  Why second: Facts without context are trivia. This section
  transforms "what happened" into "why you should care" — still
  neutrally, still without verdict.

SECTION 3: THE CONTESTED GROUND
  Purpose: Map exactly where and why people disagree
  Tone: Cartographic. Mapping, not choosing sides.
  Word count: 200–300 words
  Contains: each major interpretation or position, attributed
            to its source, with the specific claims made
  Structure: Position A says X because Y.
             Position B says X because Z.
             Position C (if exists) says...
  Does NOT contain: any signal of which position T4A favours

  Why third: Now the reader has facts (Section 1), understands
  why it matters (Section 2), and can see the full landscape of
  disagreement (Section 3). They are equipped for the analysis.

SECTION 4: WHAT THE REVIEW FOUND
  Purpose: The T4A differentiator — multi-stage analytical findings
  Tone: Forensic. Scientific report energy.
  Word count: 200–300 words (synthesis), expandable per stage
  Contains: synthesised findings from all 6 stages, areas of
            agreement, areas of disagreement, specific evidence
  Structure:
    - Synthesis paragraph (what most stages agree on)
    - Divergence paragraph (where stages disagree and why)
    - Key finding (the single most important analytical insight)

  Why fourth: This is the payoff. The reader has been equipped
  with facts, context, and the disagreement landscape. Now T4A
  delivers what no other source can — six independent analytical
  perspectives synthesised into actionable understanding.

SECTION 5: WHAT'S MISSING
  Purpose: Omission detection — the most valuable thing T4A does
  Tone: Investigative. Quiet alarm.
  Word count: 100–200 words
  Contains: specific facts, data points, or perspectives that
            the set-theoretic omission detection identified as
            present in some sources but missing from mainstream
  Structure: numbered list of omissions, each with:
    - The missing fact
    - Where it was found (which source had it)
    - Why its absence matters

  Why last: This is the mic drop. After reading everything,
  the reader learns what NOBODY ELSE TOLD THEM. This is what
  brings them back. This is T4A's addiction mechanism — and
  it's an ethical one, because the addiction is to completeness.
```

**Total article word count: 750–1,150 words** (approximately 4–6 min read)

```
│                                                     │

└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

**Per-stage content rules:**

```
STAGE          ROLE LABEL                     WHAT IT SHOWS
─────────────  ──────────────────────────────  ──────────────────────────
Stage 1        "Primary Analysis"              Comprehensive analysis
Stage 2        "Bias Audit"                    Detected bias patterns
Stage 3        "Fact Verification"             Fact-check results
Stage 4        "Alternative Framing"           Missing viewpoints identified
Stage 5        "Contrarian Stress-Test"        Adversarial challenges raised
Stage 6        "Synthesis Review"              Integration and final assessment

KEY FINDING: exactly ONE sentence per stage, 15-25 words.
This sentence must be the single most important contribution
that stage made to the analysis. If a reader reads nothing
else about this stage, this sentence must be sufficient.

Examples:
  Stage 1: "All three government data sources cite the same study,
            but each quotes different figures from it."
  Stage 2: "Headlines across all major outlets used active voice
            for government actions and passive voice for opposition."
  Stage 3: "The RM2.3B figure is technically accurate but excludes
            administrative costs included in the original report."
  Stage 4: "No major outlet included the perspective of affected
            small businesses in the implementation zone."
  Stage 5: "If the opposition's methodology is applied to the
            government's own data, the conclusion reverses."
  Stage 6: "After integrating all stage inputs, two critical
            omissions shift the overall assessment significantly."
```

## 3D. BIAS VECTOR VISUALISATION

**Purpose:** Make the mathematical framework VISIBLE and INTUITIVE.
The 12-axis radar chart is the visual signature of T4A's rigour.

**The 12 axes, with reader-friendly labels:**

```
TECHNICAL NAME        READER LABEL     WHAT IT MEASURES
────────────────────  ───────────────  ──────────────────────────
Sentiment valence     "Tone"           Positive vs negative framing
Political lean        "Political"      Left vs right positioning
Ethnic framing        "Ethnic"         Ethnic angle prominence
Religious sensitivity "Religious"      Religious framing relevance
Narrative framing     "Narrative"      Active vs passive voice bias
Omission index        "Completeness"   What's missing
Temporal bias         "Timing"         Historical context depth
Confidence inflation  "Certainty"      Overclaiming vs hedging
Source diversity      "Sources"        Range of sources consulted
Geographic bias       "Geographic"     KL-centric vs national view
Economic framing      "Economic"       Class/wealth angle
Gender agency         "Gender"         Gender voice and agency balance

READER-FRIENDLY NAMES are displayed. Technical names are available
on tap/hover for readers who want the methodology.
```

**Visual rules:**
```
- Centre point = perfectly neutral (theoretical ideal)
- Outer ring = maximum detected bias on that axis
- The filled shape shows the ACTUAL bias profile
- A perfectly neutral article would be a dot at centre
- A heavily biased article would spike on specific axes
- Shape is coloured --tfa-blue at 20% fill, 80% border
- Benchmark circle (dotted line) shows "average" for comparison
- Interactive: tap any axis to see explanation + score
```

**12-axis layout (clock positions):**
```
         Tone (12 o'clock)
    Gender          Political
  Economic            Ethnic
Geographic          Religious
  Sources            Narrative
  Certainty        Completeness
         Timing (6 o'clock)
```

**Why 12 axes and not 8:**
No major media outlet measures bias quantitatively. T4A's competitive
advantage is comprehensiveness. The 4 additional dimensions (Sources,
Geographic, Economic, Gender) are universally recognised in academic
media studies. Launching without them invites immediate criticism.
12 axes remain visually readable on a radar chart — the same
dimensionality as a clock face, which every reader already understands.

## 3E. THE OMISSION PANEL

**Purpose:** The single most valuable output of T4A.
This is what justifies the platform's existence.

**Display format:**

```
┌─ WHAT'S MISSING ─────────────────────────────────────┐
│                                                       │
│  ⚠ 2 significant facts identified as missing         │
│  from mainstream coverage of this issue.              │
│                                                       │
│  ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────┐  │
│  │ 1.                                              │  │
│  │                                                 │  │
│  │ THE FACT:                                       │  │
│  │ [Specific factual claim, 1-2 sentences]         │  │
│  │                                                 │  │
│  │ FOUND IN:                                       │  │
│  │ [Source that included this fact]                 │  │
│  │                                                 │  │
│  │ MISSING FROM:                                   │  │
│  │ [Which mainstream sources omitted it]            │  │
│  │                                                 │  │
│  │ WHY IT MATTERS:                                 │  │
│  │ [1 sentence on how this changes understanding]  │  │
│  └─────────────────────────────────────────────────┘  │
│                                                       │
│  ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────┐  │
│  │ 2.                                              │  │
│  │ [Same structure]                                │  │
│  └─────────────────────────────────────────────────┘  │
│                                                       │
│  Methodology: Set-theoretic omission detection        │
│  across [N] sources. Details →                        │
│                                                       │
└───────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

**Content rules for omissions:**

```
THE FACT:
  Must be verifiable. Must be specific.
  Not "relevant context was missing"
  But "the 2019 audit report found RM340M in discrepancies,
  which is directly relevant to the current claim."
  Word count: 15-30 words.

FOUND IN:
  Specific publication name and date.
  "Reported by [source] on [date]"

MISSING FROM:
  List the specific outlets that covered the story
  but omitted this fact. Named, not vague.
  "Absent from coverage by [outlet], [outlet], and [outlet]"

WHY IT MATTERS:
  One sentence connecting the omission to understanding.
  "Without this figure, the government's claim of fiscal
  responsibility cannot be independently verified."
  Word count: 12-20 words.
```

# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
# ELEMENT 4: PUSH NOTIFICATION
# "The tap-or-ignore moment."
# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Push notifications are the most constrained content format.
Every character must justify its existence.

**Structure:**

```
LINE 1 (TITLE):  [Category]: [Shortest possible headline]
LINE 2 (BODY):   [Score] — [One-line hook]
```

**Rules:**

```
TITLE: max 50 characters including category prefix
  Format: "POLITICS: Where RM8B Actually Went"
  The category prefix acts as a filter — readers who
  care about ECONOMY skip POLITICS notifications, etc.

BODY: max 80 characters
  Format: "78/100 — Six review stages split on the key claim"
  ALWAYS starts with the score — it's T4A's signature.
  Followed by the single most clickable fact about the analysis.

COMBINED: must make sense as a standalone micro-story.
  Reading just the notification should give the reader
  enough to decide if they need the full analysis NOW
  or can come back later.
```

**Notification frequency cap:**
- Maximum 4 push notifications per day
- Maximum 1 per 3 hours
- BREAKING override: can send immediately, but counts toward daily cap
- Readers who receive too many notifications disable them forever.
  Restraint is a feature.
